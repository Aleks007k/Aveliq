PayPal's Pay Later rate sits at 4.99% plus a fixed fee — steeper than many small businesses realize when they add buy-now-pay-later to checkout. That one line item is a useful lens for the whole Square vs. PayPal debate: both platforms look simple until you look at the fee schedule.

The choice between them comes down to what your business actually does every day. Square is built around a physical or hybrid operation with a single monthly bill. PayPal is a transaction-fee model that suits businesses living in the browser or already inside the PayPal ecosystem. Neither is universally cheaper.

## Is there a free tier?

Square has one. The Square Free plan costs $0 per month per location, and you only pay when you take a payment. The data describes it as covering "the essentials your business needs to sell in-person, online, over the phone, or out in the field." PayPal does not publish a free monthly plan, but its standard card payment rate — 2.99% plus a fixed fee — applies with no monthly subscription required. They are different models, not a free vs. paid split.

## What does Square actually cost?

Square runs three published tiers plus a custom enterprise track. The Free plan is $0 per month per location. Plus is $49 per month per location, and Premium is $149 per month per location. If you process over $250,000 per year, Square will quote you custom pricing under Square Pro rather than listing a public rate. The loyalty rewards program and advanced features only arrive at Plus and above — the quiet detail is that the free tier is genuinely useful for solo operators but starts to feel thin once you want retention tools.

Email and text marketing is included, though texts move to 3¢ each after the first 500. Square also includes banking — checking, savings, and loans — which matters for businesses that want to consolidate financial infrastructure in one place.

## What does PayPal actually cost?

PayPal's pricing is entirely transaction-based, with no monthly fee for its core payment acceptance. Standard credit and debit card payments run 2.99% plus a fixed fee. PayPal Checkout, guest checkout, Pay with Venmo, and most other commercial transactions come in at 3.49% plus a fixed fee. QR code transactions are cheaper at 2.29% plus a fixed fee. Pay Later options cost 4.99% plus a fixed fee — and that number compounds fast on larger basket sizes.

Take the hype with a little salt on PayPal's "simple" pricing story: international transactions add 1.50% on top of the domestic rate, and the Invoice Subscription Service runs $14.99 per month. ACH invoice payments through that service cost 1% capped at $10 per transaction, which is attractive for B2B businesses that can push customers toward bank transfers.

## Who should use Square?

Square fits best when you have a physical location or split your sales between in-person and online. The flat monthly structure is predictable, and the bundled tools — POS, site builder, invoicing with estimates and contracts, banking — reduce the number of separate vendors you need. I'd lean toward Square for any retail or food-service business processing below the $250,000 threshold, where the Plus plan at $49 per month per location gives you a loyalty program without getting into custom-pricing territory.

The banking integration is underrated for early-stage businesses. If you are keeping revenue in a Square checking account and drawing down a Square loan, your cash flow picture is at least in one dashboard.

## Who should use PayPal?

PayPal is the stronger choice for businesses that sell primarily online or already have customers with PayPal accounts, since friction at checkout drops when buyers don't need to enter card details. The Pay by Bank ACH option — at 1% capped at $10 per transaction — is genuinely competitive for invoicing, especially for service businesses with larger invoice amounts.

Charities that qualify for the 1.99% plus fixed fee rate (subject to eligibility and pre-approval) should pay attention here, too. And if your business touches crypto at all, PayPal handles buying, selling, and receiving crypto transfers, with no fee on incoming crypto.

The weakness is fee complexity. Once you add international buyers, Pay Later, and the subscription invoice service, reconciling your effective rate takes real work.

## The Verdict

For brick-and-mortar and hybrid businesses: Square. The flat monthly structure, the integrated POS, and the banking layer make it easier to run an operation — not just process payments.

For online-first businesses and B2B service firms that invoice: PayPal. The lower card rate at 2.99%, the cheap ACH option, and the checkout network effect tip the balance. Just budget for the international surcharge if any of your revenue crosses borders.

One thing neither platform makes easy to see upfront: the true all-in effective rate once fixed fees, plan costs, and surcharges are combined. Before you commit, run your actual monthly transaction mix through both fee schedules. The answer may surprise you — and it's yours to calculate, not mine to guess at.
