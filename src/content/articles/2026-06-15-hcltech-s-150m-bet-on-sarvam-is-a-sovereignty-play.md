HCLTech just wrote a $150 million check. The recipient is Sarvam, a Bengaluru-based AI startup now valued at $1.5 billion after closing a $234 million round, making it India's newest AI unicorn.

HCLTech led the round as the primary strategic investor, with Bessemer Venture Partners joining alongside existing backers Khosla Ventures and Peak XV Partners. The raise arrives more than two years after Sarvam collected $41 million across its seed and Series A rounds combined. Sarvam says it plans to raise a total of $300 million for this Series B.

## The Logic Behind the Deal

The investment is not purely financial. HCLTech's plan is to combine Sarvam's models with its own enterprise client relationships, engineering workforce, and software assets. Sarvam builds for India-specific languages and use cases; HCLTech has the distribution, and that pairing is the product thesis.

Sarvam's existing deployment numbers give the thesis grounding. Its conversational AI platform handles over 2 million interactions per day, its inference platform processes roughly 10 million API calls daily, and its speech models transcribe more than 500,000 hours of audio each month. A fourth system is digitizing more than 35 million pages of records. These figures reflect production contracts, not pilots — the kind of operational scale that takes time and client trust to accumulate.

The company's government footprint is also meaningful. Its multilingual voice agents have collected agricultural data from 17 million farmers for India's Ministry of Agriculture and Farmers Welfare. A separate campaign supported policy renewals for 45 million policyholders at a leading insurer. One large fintech is using Sarvam's agentic platform to support a sales force of more than 350,000 people.

## Sovereignty Is the Real Driver

The timing is not accidental. Last week, Anthropic disabled access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models after the U.S. government ordered it to suspend their use by foreign nationals, citing national security grounds. That single move illustrated precisely the risk Indian enterprises and government agencies are trying to price in.

Both OpenAI and Anthropic have described India as their second-largest market after the U.S. India consumes AI at scale. But high compute costs and limited capital have kept the field thin, leaving Sarvam among a very small group attempting to build homegrown foundation models at any serious parameter count. It launched open-source models at 30 billion and 105 billion parameters earlier this year.

## What HCLTech Gets

For HCLTech, the $150 million buys a position in the part of the AI stack it cannot easily build organically: foundation models trained on Indian languages, with government-sector deployment credentials already established. Licensing from U.S. providers now carries demonstrable policy risk, and that calculation is only getting clearer.

The fresh capital will fund research into next-generation models focused on agentic, coding, and cybersecurity applications, along with expanded compute infrastructure. Sarvam was founded by Vivek Raghavan and Pratyush Kumar, both veterans of AI4Bharat, an Indian-language AI research initiative at IIT Madras backed by technology veteran Nandan Nilekani.

India's other large IT firms will be watching this structure closely. Whether HCLTech's early move locks in a durable advantage is genuinely unclear — the sources don't say, and Infosys and Wipro's next steps are speculation. What is clear is that a price has been set for what sovereign AI infrastructure is worth to an Indian enterprise buyer.
