KPMG got caught inventing the evidence for its own pitch.

A report titled "Redefining excellence in the age of agentic AI" — published by KPMG to encourage enterprise AI adoption — contained fabricated case studies attributed to UBS, the UK's National Health Service, Swiss Federal Railways, and Transport for London. All four organizations disputed the claims. GPTZero uncovered the fabrications; the Financial Times independently verified them.

## The Sourcing Problem Underneath

The root failure appears to be careless use of AI search tools. GPTZero found that citations throughout the report are largely loose paraphrases of real sources, frequently missing URLs or correct author attributions. In some cases, no matching original source existed at all.

GPTZero calls this pattern "vibe citing." It is the same problem that earned Google a court ruling against its AI Overviews product in Germany. When a firm generating authoritative-sounding business intelligence does it, the consequences compound.

## Why It Travels

GPTZero CEO Edward Tian put the sharper point on it: reports from major consulting firms carry outsized credibility, so errors inside them spawn what he calls "secondary hallucinations." Other AI systems ingest these reports as reliable training data. Human analysts cite them without checking. A single fabricated case study in a KPMG white paper does not stay in that white paper.

KPMG has pulled the report from several websites. That is damage control, not a fix.

## The Credibility Tax

The specific embarrassment here is the symmetry. KPMG is selling AI competence while demonstrating the exact failure mode — hallucinated content dressed as research — that its clients are paying to avoid. A consulting firm's core product is judgment. A fabricated case study is not a sloppy footnote; it is a direct argument that the judgment cannot be trusted.

Enterprise clients now have a concrete reason to ask who, exactly, is reviewing the AI-generated content in the decks they are paying tens of thousands of dollars for. That question will not go away when the report does.
