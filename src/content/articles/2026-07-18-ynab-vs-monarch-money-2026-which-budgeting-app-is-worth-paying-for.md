## What Does Each App Actually Cost?

The cost lands here: YNAB charges $109 per year (billed annually, at $9.08 per month) or $14.99 per month on a rolling basis. Monarch Money charges $99 per year. On an annual commitment, Monarch is $10 cheaper.

That $10 gap is real but not the deciding factor. YNAB's monthly plan, at $14.99, runs considerably more than Monarch's annual-only price — so if you're unwilling to commit a full year upfront, YNAB costs more by a meaningful margin. Monarch publishes only an annual plan; if you want month-to-month flexibility, YNAB is your only option between the two.

Both apps charge tax where applicable on top of their listed prices.

## Is There a Free Tier Before You Commit?

YNAB offers a 34-day free trial with no credit card required. College students get a full 365-day free trial, which is the most generous offer in this category by a wide margin. Monarch gives you one week free.

If you're unsure whether zero-based budgeting will stick for you, 34 days is enough time to find out. Seven days is enough to click through the interface but probably not enough to know whether a system has changed your habits.

## What YNAB Is Actually Built For

YNAB's method is the product. Every dollar you earn gets assigned a job before you spend it — that's the zero-based approach, and the app enforces it at every step. The target-setting features, the debt management tools, and the integrated loan calculator all exist to support that methodology, not just to display your data back at you.

One feature that doesn't get enough attention: a single YNAB subscription covers up to six people. Couples, families, or close groups share one account at no added cost. For a household with two adults, that makes the $109 annual price even easier to justify.

Direct bank import works with select US, Canadian, UK, and EU banks. File-based import is also available for banks outside that network. YNAB 4 upgraders receive a 10% lifetime discount on the annual plan automatically.

## What Monarch Money Is Actually Built For

Monarch positions itself as a financial dashboard first. Budgeting by category or group is there, but so are cash flow views, financial reports, and household features that let you flag and assign transactions across a shared account. The emphasis is on seeing the complete picture of your finances, not on directing every dollar before it moves.

Monarch states it never sells your financial data and never runs ads. Those are positioning claims worth knowing, though the practical weight you assign them is a personal call.

The 7-day trial is short. If you're coming from Mint (which shut down) or another aggregation-style app, Monarch's interface will feel familiar fast — which may be exactly why one week is enough to decide.

## The Methodology Gap

Here's what the numbers show: both apps cost roughly the same per year, but they are solving different problems.

YNAB is a behavior-change tool dressed as budgeting software. It asks you to be proactive — assign money, adjust when plans change, confront the gap between what you planned and what happened. That friction is intentional. Users who engage with it tend to change how they spend; users who want a passive view of their finances tend to find it exhausting.

Monarch is a financial visibility tool with budgeting features layered in. It suits someone who wants a clear, organized view of all their accounts, spending trends, and cash flow without being prompted to rethink every purchase category. The household transaction features make it practical for couples managing finances together.

Neither model is wrong. They're just for different relationships with money.

## Which One Should You Choose?

If you want to change your spending behavior and you're willing to put in 15 minutes a week actively managing your budget, YNAB is the better fit. The zero-based system has a learning curve, but the 34-day trial is long enough to clear it. At $109 per year for up to six people, it's hard to argue the price is a barrier for a household.

If you want a clean, consolidated view of your finances — accounts, cash flow, reports — without a methodology forcing you to pre-assign every dollar, Monarch's $99 annual plan delivers that for less money. It's also the right pick if you're coming off an aggregator like Mint and want something that works similarly but better.

One scenario where the choice is easy: you're a college student. YNAB's 365-day free trial means you pay nothing for a year. Take it.

For everyone else, the honest tiebreaker is this: do you want software that holds you accountable to a plan, or software that shows you what happened? Pay for the one that matches how you'll actually use it, because an unused subscription at any price is the worst deal of the two.
